\begin{abstract}
The Nobel Prize exhibits stark geographic concentration---five countries account for over 80\% of prize-winning discoveries---but whether this reflects institutional design or individual evaluator behavior has been unknown. We construct the first multilayer network of the Nobel selection process by integrating archival nomination records with institutional composition data (8,134 individuals; 514,111 edges; five categories; 1901--1975, with Physiology/Medicine through 1953) and decompose geographic homophily across five institutional layers. Despite nearly all-Scandinavian governance, the nominator pool is geographically diverse---yet nominators select same-country candidates at 4.85 times the expected rate ($p < 0.001$): 46.8\% of nominations pair same-country individuals, versus 9.6\% expected under random pairing. This concentration is strongest in Literature (8.58$\times$) and weakest in Physics (3.01$\times$), a gradient consistent with disciplines where evaluation depends on shared language and cultural context versus those with more standardized technical criteria. The absolute excess same-geography rate rose through the 1940s and remains elevated despite diversification of the nominee pool, indicating that expanding candidate diversity alone has not produced proportional changes in nomination behavior. By localizing geographic sorting to the discretionary nomination decision rather than formal institutional design, these findings identify a specific intervention point for reducing geographic concentration in scientific recognition systems.
\end{abstract}

\noindent The Nobel Prize is the most visible recognition system in science \cite{CasFan2013}, yet men from Europe or North America have received the vast majority of prizes \cite{LunJenJau2019, ChaEllRah2011, Cra2001}, and just five countries account for over 80\% of prize-winning discoveries \cite{vonZed2025}. Geographic concentration reinforces cumulative advantage \cite{Mer1968}: dominant regions accumulate resources, talent, and further recognition \cite{Zuc1977, ColCol1973, Gar1972}, while racial and national disparities compound the pattern \cite{NeiVueVue2024}. Whether these disparities reflect selection processes or the geographic distribution of scientific output has been unknown---yet the answer determines whether reform should target evaluator behavior or the pipeline of candidates. Homophily---the tendency for similar individuals to form ties \cite{McPSmoCoo2001}---offers a way to localize the source: comparing observed same-geography rates to expected rates at each stage of a selection chain reveals where geographic sorting enters.

The architecture of the Nobel system offers a unique opportunity to localize the source of geographic sorting. The process comprises multiple layers: governing bodies that set policy, vetting committees that solicit nominators, nominators who submit candidates, and deliberating committees that select laureates \cite{methods}. Each layer could introduce or amplify concentration. Prior work on the Nobel nomination network shows that nominations cluster by country \cite{GalDom2019}, that same-country peer reviewers recommend acceptance more often \cite{ZumTep2025}, and that laureates occupy central positions in collaborative networks \cite{WagHorWhe2015}. Gendered barriers further constrain who enters the nomination pipeline \cite{ModGilSha2018, LunJenJau2019}. But single-layer analyses of the nominator--nominee relationship \cite{GalDom2019} cannot determine whether observed clustering reflects nominator preferences or an institutionally skewed nominator pool---if the all-Scandinavian committees disproportionately invited European nominators, the resulting nominations would cluster by country even absent individual geographic preference. Regression-based approaches \cite{KoJanYan2024} face a similar identification problem.

We address this gap by decomposing the selection process into a multilayer network \cite{Kivela2014} with separate edge sets for each decision point. If the all-Scandinavian committees were the primary source of geographic sorting, we would expect it to manifest as a geographically skewed nominator pool---for example, committees disproportionately inviting Scandinavian nominators. If nominators were the primary source, we would expect a diverse nominator pool but concentration in the nominations those individuals make. We assess which pattern the data support using two complementary approaches: comparing the geographic composition of each layer and computing homophily ratios for each edge type. Although interdependencies exist between layers---committee composition shapes the available nominator pool, for instance---our focus on direct edge homophily provides a clear, interpretable decomposition of the selection chain.

\subsection*{Network Construction and Homophily Measurement}

We construct a directed multilayer network spanning all five original Nobel Prize categories---Chemistry, Physics, Physiology/Medicine, Literature, and Peace---from 1901 to 1974--1975, the most recent publicly available years under the Swedish legal 50-year secrecy rule for official nomination archives (Fig.~\ref{fig:schematic}). Temporal coverage varies by prize: Physiology/Medicine nomination records extend only through 1953 because the Karolinska Institutet has not released later records; institutional-layer data extend further. We exclude the Economics Prize (established 1969) because of its different institutional structure and limited archival overlap. The network has five layers: governing bodies, vetting bodies, nominators, nominees, and laureates. We model institutional relationships as complete bipartite connections---every member of one body linked to every member of the next---within each year and prize category, reflecting their collective nature. This construction means that institutional homophily ratios primarily reflect endpoint composition rather than selective pairing, a point we return to below. Nomination edges connect nominator--nominee pairs where a nomination occurred. We draw geographic data from two distinct sources: the nomination layer uses country of professional activity from the archival record, while the institutional layers use birth country from Wikidata. The robustness check using birth country for nomination edges, which yields a ratio of 3.82, confirms that the pattern holds under an alternative geographic measure. We assembled the network from archival records, biographical databases, and scholarly literature spanning 81 countries; it comprises 8,134 individuals and 514,111 directed edges: 26,654 nomination and 487,457 institutional. For each edge type, we compute the homophily ratio at three geographic scales: country, subregion---geographic clusters such as Northern Europe, Southern Europe, Northern America, Southern America, and various Asian regions---and continent. We compute the ratio by dividing the fraction of same-geography edges by the fraction expected under random pairing. A ratio of 1.0 indicates geographic neutrality; values above 1.0 indicate geographic concentration. This null model is a statistical benchmark for detecting excess same-geography pairing given the observed marginal distributions, not a claim that scientific capacity is geographically uniform; a ratio exceeding 1.0 could reflect geographic differences in research visibility, evaluator preference, or both. A ratio of 4.85 means nominators and nominees shared a country 4.85 times more often than expected by chance; the corresponding Newman assortativity $r = 0.41$ confirms strong sorting under either measure. We assess significance with 10,000-fold permutation tests that shuffle target geographies across edges while holding source geographies fixed, preserving the marginal geographic distributions of each \cite{methods}. This tests whether observed same-geography rates exceed what those marginal distributions alone would produce; we supplement the pooled test with stratified analyses by discipline and decade in the supplement, where full construction details, subregion definitions, and sensitivity checks also appear.

\subsection*{Homophily Concentrates at the Nomination Edge}

The governing and vetting bodies are nearly entirely European (predominantly Scandinavian), at 97.6\% and 98.9\% respectively, while the nominator, nominee, and laureate layers are more diverse, with 65.7--71.3\% European representation. The effective number of subregions---a diversity measure where higher values indicate representation across more geographic regions---ranges from 5.80 to 6.30 for the nominator, nominee, and laureate layers, compared with 1.23--1.65 for institutional layers. Despite this compositional contrast, geographic homophily concentrates in a single edge type: the nominator $\to$ nominee relationship (Fig.~\ref{fig:edge_homophily}).

At the country level, the nominator $\to$ nominee homophily ratio is 4.85, with $p < 0.001$: nominators and their nominees share the same country nearly five times more often than expected. The 95\% null reference interval under random pairing is [0.97, 1.03]---narrow because of the large sample of 25,844 edges---so the observed ratio lies far outside the range expected under geographic independence. The observed same-country rate is 46.8\%, versus 9.6\% expected. For context, geographic homophily in social networks is pervasive but typically moderate \cite{McPSmoCoo2001}, and recent work on journal peer review finds that same-country reviewers recommend acceptance at moderately elevated rates \cite{ZumTep2025}; a ratio of 4.85 indicates exceptionally strong geographic clustering by comparison. A robustness check using birth country from Wikidata, available for 27.3\% of individuals, yields a ratio of 3.82 with $p < 0.001$. This is lower, as expected, but still far above neutral. This matched subset is not a random sample---individuals with Wikidata pages are disproportionately prominent---so its homophily characteristics may differ from the full population. The primary finding survives Bonferroni correction across 49 independent tests, giving $\alpha_{\mathrm{adjusted}} = 0.05/49 \approx 0.001$. Secondary findings at the discipline and decade level should be interpreted with appropriate caution.

Institutional edge types show ratios near 1.0 at the continent level: governing body $\to$ laureate at 1.01, vetting body $\to$ nominator at 1.00 with $p = 1.000$, and vetting body $\to$ laureate at 1.00 with $p = 1.000$. As expected from the complete bipartite construction, these near-1.0 ratios serve as a compositional sanity check rather than a direct test of institutional selection neutrality. At the country level, most institutional edges similarly cluster near 1.0. The one exception is the governing body $\to$ vetting body edge, which has a country-level ratio of 1.65. This arises because we pool edges across multiple prize-awarding institutions (RSAS, Karolinska, Swedish Academy, Storting), each of which appoints its own national committee members, so same-country rates in the pooled edge set exceed what the combined marginal distributions alone would predict.

Stronger evidence comes from the \emph{composition} of the nominator pool. Despite the vetting body being almost entirely Scandinavian, the nominator pool is geographically diverse, with an effective number of subregions of 6.30 compared to 1.23 for the vetting body. Institutional solicitation did not bottleneck geographic diversity---sorting occurs one step later, when those diverse nominators choose whom to nominate.

\subsection*{Homophily Varies Across Disciplines}

Geographic homophily varies across prize categories. At the country level, Literature exhibits the strongest homophily at 8.58, all $p < 0.001$, followed by Peace at 5.26, Physiology/Medicine at 5.16, Chemistry at 3.55, and Physics at 3.01. Because Physiology/Medicine data end in 1953 while other prizes extend to 1974--1975, and because homophily changes over time, we verified this ranking on a harmonized 1901--1953 window. The broad ordering is preserved---Literature highest (9.81), the physical sciences lowest (Chemistry 3.48, Physics 3.37)---with Physiology/Medicine and Peace essentially tied in the middle (5.16 and 5.15). The ordering tracks the degree to which evaluation in each field depends on shared language and cultural context: literary merit is inseparable from the language in which a work is written, while assessment in physics and chemistry relies more heavily on standardized technical criteria---though even these fields retain geographic structure in training, collaboration, and professional networks. Within-subregion nomination rates reveal geographic heterogeneity: in Literature, Southern European nominators direct 92.7\% of their nominations within Southern Europe, while in Physics the figure drops to 24.9\%. Northern American nominators show intermediate behavior, directing 81.4\% of their Physiology/Medicine nominations within Northern America but only 44.4\% in Literature. Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap} displays these rates by prize and subregion.

\subsection*{Temporal Dynamics: Homophily Rises and Persists Despite Diversification}

The nominee pool diversified substantially: the European share fell from 94.2\% in the 1900s to 55.7\% in the 1970s, and the effective number of continents rose from 1.28 to 2.52. Fig.~\ref{fig:temporal}, panel~A, and Table~S5 display these trends. Because Physiology/Medicine nomination data end in 1953, the set of prizes contributing to the aggregate time series changes after that point; analyses of individual prize categories in the supplement confirm that each shows rising continent-level homophily. Despite this diversification, the continent-level homophily ratio \emph{increased}, from 1.07 in the 1900s to 1.54 in the 1940s, stabilizing at 1.43--1.45 through the 1970s, all with $p < 0.001$. Fig.~\ref{fig:temporal}, panel~B, shows this trend.

This pattern arises partly from the mechanics of the ratio: the absolute same-continent nomination rate decreased from 95.6\% to 65.6\%, but the expected rate under random pairing decreased faster, from 89.0\% to 45.7\%. As a result, $H = O/E$ rises even if nominators' underlying geographic preferences remain constant. In the early decades, the nominee pool was so overwhelmingly European that nearly any nomination would pair same-continent individuals by chance, leaving little room for large homophily ratios. A complementary absolute measure, $O - E$ (the absolute excess same-continent rate), provides additional evidence: $O - E$ rose from 6.6 percentage points in the 1900s to 27.0 in the 1940s before declining to 19.9 by the 1970s (Table~S5). The rise through the 1940s indicates that the increasing $H$ during that period reflects a genuine widening of the gap between observed and expected rates, not merely a shrinking denominator. The post-1940s decline in $O - E$, combined with a stable $H$, confirms that the later plateau in $H$ is partly a ratio artifact: observed and expected rates fell in approximate proportion, keeping $H$ roughly constant even as the absolute excess narrowed. The 1940s peak coincides with World War II disruptions to international scientific communication, which may have amplified geographic clustering in available information about candidates. This elevated ratio persisted from the 1950s onward even as international science recovered, suggesting that wartime constraints may have had lasting effects on nomination networks. Each prize category individually shows rising continent-level homophily, with the sharpest increases in the natural sciences during the 1930s--1940s.

\subsection*{Discussion}

Without observing each layer separately, it is impossible to distinguish nominator preference from institutional gatekeeping: if institutions preferentially invited European nominators, nominations would cluster by country even absent individual geographic preference. Our multilayer decomposition resolves this ambiguity through two independent lines of evidence. First, the nominator pool is geographically diverse despite being assembled by nearly all-Scandinavian institutions---effective number of subregions 6.30, versus 1.23 for the vetting body---establishing that institutional solicitation does not bottleneck diversity. Second, within that diverse pool, nominators show strong same-country preference: 4.85 at the country level, 1.43--1.54 at the continent level. Because we model institutional edges as complete bipartite connections, their near-1.0 ratios confirm compositional expectations but cannot rule out subtler institutional gatekeeping; a more targeted test would require data on individual committee members' votes or influence. The stronger claim is compositional: the institutions assembled a diverse nominator pool, and it is within that pool that geographic concentration appears. A stronger test would require the invitation lists themselves---who was asked to nominate, not just who submitted---but these records are not publicly available.

What drives nominators to favor geographically proximate nominees? Academic evaluators rely on disciplinary norms, personal networks, and tacit knowledge of what counts as excellence \cite{Lam2009}, and all three channels are geographically structured. Three mechanisms are plausible. First, \emph{visibility}: knowledge flows are geographically localized even after controlling for the spatial distribution of activity \cite{JafTraHen1993}, so nominators may simply be more familiar with work produced nearby. Second, \emph{epistemic affinity}: nominators may regard work in their own tradition as higher quality, not from conscious partiality but because their evaluative frameworks were shaped by the same intellectual context. Third, \emph{strategic nationalism}: nominators may advocate for compatriots to enhance national scientific prestige. Our data cannot distinguish among these mechanisms, but the discipline gradient provides suggestive evidence. Literature, where evaluation depends on shared language and cultural context, shows the strongest homophily at 8.58$\times$, while physics and chemistry, where standardized technical criteria play a larger role, show the weakest at 3.01$\times$ and 3.55$\times$. This gradient is more consistent with visibility and epistemic affinity than with strategic nationalism, which would not obviously vary across disciplines, though other confounds such as nomination rules (Literature and Peace allow open nominations, whereas the sciences are invitation-only), field size, and the number of eligible candidates could produce similar gradients. It is also consistent with the geography of knowledge production: the spatial organization of intellectual activity shapes what scientists encounter \cite{Livingstone2003, Sha1994}, and stratification in science operates through cumulative small selection decisions \cite{ColCol1973, Mer1968, deSollaPrice1963}.

One partial corrective operates at the aggregate level: the median laureate received nominations from four distinct countries, with a range of 1 to 20 (computed from the nomination archive; see data repository \cite{nobelgithub}), so the system's reliance on many nominators dilutes any single geographic preference. Nevertheless, geographic homophily generates systematic imbalances in the flow of recognition, as shown in Fig.~S1. Northern America is the largest net importer of nominations by volume, with a return ratio (nominations received / nominations sent) of 1.44, while Northern Europe---the administering region---is the largest net exporter by volume, with a return ratio of 0.73.

Several limitations warrant emphasis. The 50-year secrecy rule restricts our data to 1901--1975; we cannot determine whether patterns have persisted, though the temporal trend shows no sign of diminishing by the end of the observation window. The underlying mechanisms---information asymmetry, professional networks, cumulative advantage---have not disappeared from science, and recent evidence of geographic sorting in peer review \cite{ZumTep2025} suggests these patterns extend beyond the Nobel system and beyond the mid-twentieth century. Our vetting body $\to$ nominator analysis assumes that response rates to nomination invitations do not vary systematically by geography; differential non-response could either inflate or deflate the observed diversity of the nominator pool, and because we observe only the realized pool, the direction of this potential bias on the nominator $\to$ nominee homophily ratio is uncertain. Worst-case sensitivity bounds remain far above the permutation null: assuming all 2,114 records with missing geography are cross-country yields $H = 4.48$; treating all 432 historically recoded same-country matches as spurious yields $H = 4.68$ (see supplement). As discussed in the temporal analysis, the ratio $H = O/E$ is mechanically sensitive to $E$, and the complementary absolute measure $O - E$ (Table~S5) confirms that the post-1940s plateau in $H$ partly reflects ratio mechanics rather than sustained behavioral intensification. Our analysis measures geographic sorting---not bias per se. Homophily patterns could partly reflect geographic differences in research visibility, information access, or institutional networks rather than preference bias; separating these mechanisms would require external data on research output by geography, beyond the scope of this study. Finally, we focus on geography to the exclusion of race, gender, and ethnicity. Geography is the one identity dimension for which we have near-complete data across all layers, but it is not the only axis of inequality, and geographic disparities may partly proxy for racial, linguistic, or institutional hierarchies that we cannot disentangle with these data. Although we report gender composition by layer in Table~S6, female representation is too low (2.3--11.1\%) to support gender-based homophily analyses. Race and ethnicity are not systematically recorded in historical sources, and the categories themselves are socially constructed differently across cultures and periods, making retrospective classification unreliable. Gender and racial disparities in the Nobel system are well documented \cite{LunJenJau2019, NeiVueVue2024} and warrant separate investigation with appropriate data.

The Nobel Prize shapes careers, funding, and public perceptions of scientific excellence \cite{CasFan2013, But2016}, so identifying where geographic sorting enters the selection process has consequences beyond the prize itself. Expanding the geographic diversity of the nominee pool alone is insufficient if nomination behavior does not change proportionally \cite{Nature2022}. The geographic clustering we observe suggests that evaluation systems in which individual experts exercise judgment---science prizes, funding panels, editorial boards---may be susceptible to geographic concentration even when the evaluators themselves are geographically diverse. Because geographic sorting concentrates at the discretionary nomination decision rather than in formal institutional design, reducing geographic concentration requires interventions targeting nominator behavior---structured nomination procedures, explicit instructions to consider international candidates, cognitive debiasing---alongside continued efforts to diversify who is invited to nominate. The multilayer decomposition we introduce is transferable to other recognition systems---funding panels, editorial boards, professional awards---wherever the question is whether geographic sorting arises from institutional policy or individual evaluator behavior.

\paragraph*{Ethics and positionality:} This study analyzes publicly archived Nobel Prize nomination records and published biographical data; no human subjects research was conducted. We acknowledge that the authors represent institutions and nations well-represented in the Nobel system (US and Sweden), which may shape our interpretation and introduce blind spots---particularly regarding how geographic exclusion is experienced in underrepresented regions, and how geographic patterns intersect with racial, gender, and linguistic inequalities we do not examine. We explicitly caution against interpreting findings of geographic homophily as evidence that geographic outsiders are inherently less deserving of recognition. Rather, our results identify a structural pattern that should motivate institutional reforms to equalize access to recognition globally. Geographic clustering in nominations reflects the cumulative effects of past recognition patterns, which institutions can actively counteract through diversified nominator recruitment and structured nomination procedures.
