\begin{abstract}
\bfseries \boldmath
In the Nobel Prize selection process, where does geographic bias originate --- in the Scandinavian institutions that govern the system, or in the scientists who nominate? We construct the first multilayer network of the process (8,134 individuals, 303,629 edges, five prize categories, 1901--1975) and decompose homophily across five layers. Bias enters at a single point: nominators select same-country nominees nearly five times more often than expected (ratio 4.85, $p < 0.001$); institutional edges show ratios near 1.0. This homophily is strongest in Literature (8.58), weakest in Physics (3.01), and has intensified over time even as the nominee pool diversified. Diversifying the candidate pool, without changing how evaluators exercise judgment, is insufficient to reduce geographic bias.
\end{abstract}

\noindent The Nobel Prize is the most visible recognition system in science, yet the process by which laureates are selected remains only partially understood. Since 1901, the vast majority of laureates have been men from Europe or North America \cite{LunJenJau2019, Fis2013, Cra2001}, and over 80\% of prize-winning discoveries were made in just five countries \cite{vonZed2025}. This geographic concentration compounds through what Merton \cite{Mer1968} termed the ``Matthew effect'': prestige begets further prestige, concentrating resources and talent in regions that already dominate \cite{OysBybTer2006, WilMilKas2021}. Understanding where geographic sorting enters the system is therefore prerequisite to any effort to correct it.

Prior work has established that geographic homophily shapes Nobel nominations: nominators favor individuals from similar national backgrounds \cite{GalDom2019}, and wealthy nations disproportionately nominate their own citizens \cite{KoJanYan2024, BafVam2011}. Dumlao and Teplitskiy \cite{DumTep2025} showed that same-country peer reviewers are more likely to recommend acceptance, suggesting geographic sorting is systemic in scientific evaluation. But a fundamental question remains: at which stage of the selection pipeline does this sorting enter? The answer determines where reform can be effective. Gallotti and De Domenico's \cite{GalDom2019} single-layer, single-discipline design could not distinguish institutional sources from individual ones. Ko et al.'s \cite{KoJanYan2024} regression approach could not decompose the hierarchy connecting governing bodies, vetting committees, nominators, nominees, and laureates. Answering the ``where'' question requires a multilayer network that represents each layer and decision type as a separate edge --- a structure not previously constructed for any scientific prize system.

We construct this network (Figure~\ref{fig:schematic}), spanning all five prize categories from 1901 to 1975 (the most recent publicly available year). Five institutional layers --- governing bodies, vetting bodies, nominators, nominees, and laureates --- are represented as distinct vertex sets connected by directed edges encoding each institutional decision \cite{Kivela2014}. Assembled from over a dozen archival and biographical sources spanning 81 countries and eight decades, the network yields 8,134 individuals and 303,629 directed edges. This structure allows us to decompose geographic homophily by edge type, isolating where geographic sorting enters the selection process \cite{methods}.

\subsection*{Layer Composition}

Table~\ref{tab:layer_composition} summarizes the composition of each layer. The governing and vetting bodies are nearly entirely European (97.6\% and 98.9\%), reflecting the Scandinavian institutional design of the Nobel system --- Swedish for four prizes, Norwegian for Peace. The nominator, nominee, and laureate layers are substantially more diverse (65.7--71.3\% European, effective subregion counts of 5.80--6.30 compared to 1.23--1.65 for the institutional layers). This contrast frames our central question: does the geographic homogeneity of the institutional layers propagate forward as bias in nominations, or does the sorting originate elsewhere?

\subsection*{Geographic Homophily at the Nomination Edge}

Geographic homophily in the Nobel Prize network is concentrated in a single edge type: the nominator $\to$ nominee relationship. We measure homophily as the ratio of the observed same-geography rate to the rate expected under random pairing within each edge type, with significance assessed by permuting geographic labels across individuals (1,000 iterations) while preserving network topology \cite{methods}. Figure~\ref{fig:edge_homophily} and table~S4 present these ratios for all five edge types at the country level.

At the country level, the nominator $\to$ nominee homophily ratio is 4.85 ($p < 0.001$): nominators and their nominees share the same country nearly five times more often than expected under random pairing. The observed same-country rate is 46.8\%, compared to an expected rate of 9.6\%. This estimate uses the country of professional activity recorded in the nomination archive, which tracks institutional affiliation at the time of each nomination. As a robustness check, we re-estimate homophily using birth country from Wikidata for the QID-matched subset: the ratio is 3.82 ($p < 0.001$), lower --- as expected, since some individuals work in countries other than their country of birth --- but still strongly significant. The result is thus robust to the choice of geographic identifier.

By contrast, institutional edge types show homophily ratios near 1.00 at the continent level: governing body $\to$ laureate (1.01), vetting body $\to$ nominator (1.00), and vetting body $\to$ laureate (1.00). The one exception is governing body $\to$ vetting body, which shows a country-level ratio of 1.94. However, this reflects a structural tautology rather than a discretionary preference: vetting body members are selected \emph{from} the governing body, and both bodies are almost entirely Scandinavian, so the high same-country rate is an artifact of institutional design rather than an independent source of geographic sorting. At the continent level, this edge type also shows a ratio of 1.00.

The vetting body $\to$ nominator edge is particularly informative. Despite the vetting body being almost entirely Scandinavian, the nominators they invite to participate are geographically diverse (71.3\% European, effective subregion count 6.30). The same-country homophily ratio for this edge type is 1.01 ($p = 0.070$, not statistically significant). In other words, the institutional machinery of the Nobel system does not preferentially channel invitations toward geographically proximate scientists. The geographic sorting enters the system one step later, when those nominators choose whom to nominate.

\subsection*{Homophily Varies Across Disciplines}

Geographic homophily varies across the five Nobel Prize categories in a pattern consistent with each discipline's degree of international integration. Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} displays within-subregion nomination rates by prize and subregion, revealing the discipline gradient at a glance.

At the country level, the Nobel Prize in Literature exhibits the strongest homophily (ratio 8.58, $p < 0.001$), followed by Peace (5.26), Physiology/Medicine (5.16), Chemistry (3.55), and Physics (3.01). This ordering is interpretable: literature is bound to language, which correlates with geography, while physics and chemistry deal in mathematical formalisms that are internationally legible. The Peace Prize occupies a middle position, reflecting its mix of nationally prominent political figures and internationally recognized activists.

Within-subregion nomination rates sharpen this picture. In Literature, Southern European nominators direct 92.7\% of their nominations to Southern European nominees, and Eastern European nominators direct 85.7\% to Eastern European nominees. In Physics, the corresponding rates are only 24.9\% and 41.3\%. Northern American nominators direct 81.4\% of their nominations to other Northern American nominees in Physiology/Medicine and 76.8\% in Physics, but only 44.4\% in Literature, where they look outward more than in the sciences.

\subsection*{The Globalization Paradox}

The nominee pool diversified substantially over the 20th century: the European share fell from 94.2\% in the 1900s to 55.7\% in the 1970s, and the effective number of continents rose from 1.28 to 2.52 (Figure~\ref{fig:temporal}, panel~A; table~S5). Despite this diversification, the homophily ratio \emph{increased}, from 1.07 in the 1900s to a peak of 1.54 in the 1940s, stabilizing at 1.43--1.45 through the 1970s (Figure~\ref{fig:temporal}, panel~B).

This pattern arises because the absolute same-continent nomination rate decreased (from 95.6\% to 65.6\%) but the expected rate under random pairing decreased faster (from 89.0\% to 45.7\%). In the early decades, the nominee pool was so overwhelmingly European that nearly any nomination would pair same-continent individuals by chance alone, leaving little room for detectable homophily. As the pool diversified, the expected same-continent rate fell, but nomination behavior did not diversify proportionally --- the gap between observed and expected widened. The 1940s peak likely reflects the geographic constraints of World War II, which restricted international scientific communication and may have further concentrated nominations within regions.

The Nobel system has thus become more globally inclusive in absolute terms while becoming more geographically biased in relative terms. The paradox holds within each prize category individually: all five show rising continent-level homophily ratios over the study period, with the sharpest increases in the natural sciences during the 1930s--1940s, consistent with the geographic constraints of wartime. The same-continent preference was always present; it became measurable only as the nominee pool grew diverse enough to provide a non-trivial null expectation.

\subsection*{Discussion}

The most striking result is what we do \emph{not} find. The nearly all-Scandinavian institutions that govern the Nobel system show no detectable geographic preference --- neither in whom they invite to nominate (ratio 1.01, $p = 0.070$) nor in whom they select as laureates (ratio 1.01). Bias enters entirely through the nominators themselves: scientists selected through a geographically neutral process who then exercise their discretion in a geographically biased way. This is consistent with Dumlao and Teplitskiy \cite{DumTep2025}, who find that same-country peer reviewers are more likely to recommend acceptance --- geographic sorting in evaluation appears to be systemic rather than institutional.

What drives nominators to favor geographically proximate nominees? Three mechanisms are plausible: \emph{visibility} (scientists are more familiar with work produced in their own country and presented at their own conferences), \emph{epistemic affinity} (nominators' evaluative frameworks are shaped by their own intellectual context), and \emph{strategic nationalism} (advocacy for compatriots to enhance national prestige). Some degree of geographic correlation may also reflect the nomination system working as intended: nominators are best positioned to evaluate work they know firsthand, which is disproportionately local. Our data cannot fully distinguish these mechanisms, but the discipline gradient is suggestive. Literature, bound to language and national traditions, shows the strongest homophily (8.58$\times$), while physics and chemistry, whose formalisms are internationally legible, show the weakest (3.01$\times$ and 3.55$\times$). This gradient is more consistent with visibility and epistemic affinity than with strategic nationalism, which would not obviously vary across disciplines.

One partial corrective operates at the aggregate level: the median laureate in our data received nominations from four distinct countries (range 1--20), so the system's reliance on many independent nominators dilutes any single nominator's geographic preference. Nevertheless, these nomination patterns generate systematic imbalances in the flow of recognition (fig.~S1). Northern America is the largest net importer of nominations (return ratio 1.44), while Northern Europe --- the region that administers the Nobel system --- is the largest net exporter (return ratio 0.73). In the framework of Merton \cite{Mer1968}, this constitutes a geographic dimension of cumulative advantage: regions that already concentrate scientific prestige receive a disproportionate share of nominations, reinforcing the very concentration that produced the imbalance.

The temporal finding has direct policy implications. Expanding the geographic diversity of the nominee pool alone is insufficient if nomination behavior does not change proportionally: the observed same-country nomination rate is 46.8\%, versus 9.6\% expected under geography-neutral pairing, a gap of 37 percentage points. Structural reforms --- geographically stratified nomination quotas, explicit instructions to consider international candidates, or de-biasing training for invited nominators --- could address the source of the homophily we document. Whether such reforms are desirable is a question of scientific governance that our data inform but do not resolve. More broadly, any evaluation system in which a geographically concentrated group of experts exercises discretionary judgment --- science prizes, national academies, funding panels, editorial boards --- is susceptible to the same dynamic.

\subsection*{Limitations}

Several limitations constrain our analysis. First, the nomination archive is subject to a 50-year secrecy rule, so our data extend only through the mid-1970s. We cannot determine whether the patterns we document have persisted, intensified, or diminished in subsequent decades, though the post-1975 expansion of international scientific collaboration, the digitization of scholarly communication, and the growing globalization of research funding suggest that both the nominee pool and nomination behavior may have continued to shift. Second, our entity resolution matched only 27.3\% of nomination archive individuals to Wikidata QIDs (86.5\% of those with birth years). While the full nomination dataset preserves the topological structure of nominator--nominee relationships and provides per-nomination geographic fields at 94.7--97.7\% completeness, cross-layer analyses are limited to the matched subset. Third, the primary analysis uses the country of professional activity recorded in the nomination archive, while the QID-matched robustness check uses birth country from Wikidata; neither captures migration trajectories, dual nationality, or historical changes in national boundaries (particularly relevant for territories that changed sovereignty during the study period). Fourth, our measure of homophily is relative to a null model of random pairing; the appropriate normative benchmark for ``how much homophily is too much'' is a question our statistical framework does not address.

\subsection*{Conclusions}

By decomposing the Nobel Prize selection process into a multilayer network, we show that geographic sorting enters at a single point --- the discretionary nomination decision --- rather than through institutional design. The challenge is not whom institutions invite to participate, but how individual evaluators exercise their judgment once invited. The globalization paradox we document carries a broader warning: in any evaluation system, diversifying the candidate pool without changing evaluator behavior may create the appearance of inclusion while leaving --- or even widening --- the underlying bias.
