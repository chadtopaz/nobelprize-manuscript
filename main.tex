\begin{abstract}
\bfseries \boldmath
The Nobel Prize exhibits stark geographic concentration---five countries account for over 80\% of prize-winning discoveries---but whether this reflects institutional design or individual evaluator behavior has been unknown. By integrating archival nomination records with institutional composition data, we construct the first multilayer network of the Nobel selection process (8,134 individuals, 303,629 edges, five categories, 1901--1975), decomposing geographic homophily across five institutional layers. Despite nearly all-Scandinavian governance, the nominator pool is geographically diverse, yet nominators favor same-country candidates at 4.85 times the expected rate ($p < 0.001$). This concentration is strongest in Literature (8.58$\times$), weakest in Physics (3.01$\times$), and has risen and persisted over time despite diversification of the nominee pool. Expanding candidate diversity alone has not produced proportional changes in nomination behavior.
\end{abstract}

\noindent The Nobel Prize is the most visible recognition system in science \cite{CasFan2013}, yet the vast majority of laureates have been men from Europe or North America \cite{LunJenJau2019, ChaEllRah2011, Cra2001}, and just five countries account for over 80\% of prize-winning discoveries \cite{vonZed2025}. Racial and national disparities compound this geographic concentration \cite{NeiVueVue2024}. Geographic concentration reinforces cumulative advantage \cite{Mer1968}: prestige attracts further prestige, and dominant regions accumulate resources, talent, and further recognition \cite{Zuc1977, ColCol1973}. However, it remains unknown whether these patterns reflect selection processes or simply the geographic distribution of scientific output.

Homophily---the tendency for similar individuals to form ties---is a fundamental principle in social networks \cite{McPSmoCoo2001}. We quantify geographic clustering by comparing observed same-geography nomination rates to those expected under a null model in which geography and nomination are independent. This null model is a statistical benchmark, not a claim that scientific capacity is geographically uniform; research capacity, visibility, and infrastructure vary across regions for well-documented historical reasons. A ratio exceeding 1.0 could reflect these real geographic differences in output, or it could reflect evaluator preference for geographically proximate scholars, or both. Even if geographic differences in output are real, preferential nomination compounds existing inequalities. Regardless of mechanism, the result is the same: the Nobel Prize selection process amplifies existing geographic disparities in scientific visibility and resources. Disentangling the source matters for two reasons. First, \emph{fairness}: scientists from underrepresented regions deserve equitable access to nomination and recognition. Second, \emph{epistemic integrity}: evaluation systems that preferentially amplify geographically proximate work miss contributions from elsewhere and produce distorted records of scientific progress.

The architecture of the Nobel system offers a unique opportunity to localize the source of geographic sorting. The process comprises multiple layers: governing bodies that set policy, vetting committees that solicit nominators, nominators who submit candidates, and deliberating committees that select laureates \cite{methods}. Each layer could introduce or amplify concentration. Prior work shows that nominators favor individuals from similar national backgrounds \cite{GalDom2019}, that same-country peer reviewers recommend acceptance more often \cite{DumTep2025}, and that laureates occupy central positions in collaborative networks \cite{WagHorWhe2015}. Gendered barriers further constrain who enters the nomination pipeline \cite{ModGilSha2018, LunJenJau2019}. But existing analyses---single-layer \cite{GalDom2019} or regression-based \cite{KoJanYan2024}---could not distinguish institutional sources from individual ones or isolate each layer's independent contribution.

We address this gap by decomposing the selection process into a multilayer network \cite{Kivela2014} with separate edge sets for each decision point. This decomposition allows us to localize where geographic concentration appears in the system. If the all-Scandinavian committees were the primary source of geographic sorting, we would expect it to manifest as a geographically skewed nominator pool---for example, committees disproportionately inviting Scandinavian nominators. If nominators were the primary source, we would expect a diverse nominator pool but concentration in the nominations those individuals make. We assess which pattern the data support using two complementary approaches: comparing the geographic composition of each layer (which does not depend on edge modeling choices) and computing homophily ratios for each edge type. Although interdependencies exist between layers---committee composition shapes the available nominator pool, for instance---our focus on direct edge homophily provides a clear, interpretable decomposition of the selection chain. We note that our analysis examines each edge type independently rather than modeling inter-layer coupling; future work could use coupled multilayer null models to assess how homophily in one layer constrains outcomes in others.

\subsection*{Network Construction and Homophily Measurement}

We construct a directed multilayer network spanning all five original Nobel Prize categories---Chemistry, Physics, Physiology/Medicine, Literature, and Peace---from 1901 to 1974--1975, the most recent publicly available years under the Swedish legal 50-year secrecy rule for official nomination archives (Fig.~\ref{fig:schematic}). Temporal coverage varies by prize: Physiology/Medicine nomination records extend only through 1953 because the Karolinska Institutet has not released later records; institutional-layer data extend further. We exclude the Sveriges Riksbank Prize in Economic Sciences, established in 1969, because it operates under a different institutional structure and its short overlap with the archival window provides insufficient data. The network has five layers: governing bodies, vetting bodies, nominators, nominees, and laureates. We model institutional relationships as complete bipartite connections---every member of one body linked to every member of the next---within each year and prize category, reflecting their collective nature. This construction means that institutional homophily ratios primarily reflect endpoint composition rather than selective pairing, a point we return to below. Nomination edges connect nominator--nominee pairs where a nomination occurred. An important distinction separates the two geographic data sources: nomination-layer geography uses country of professional activity from the archival record, while institutional-layer geography uses birth country from Wikidata. The robustness check using birth country for nomination edges, which yields a ratio of 3.82, partially addresses this difference by confirming that the pattern holds under an alternative geographic measure. We assembled the network from archival records, biographical databases, and scholarly literature spanning 81 countries. It comprises 8,134 individuals and 303,629 directed edges: 26,654 nomination and 276,975 institutional. For each edge type, we compute the homophily ratio at three geographic scales: country, subregion---geographic clusters such as Northern Europe, Southern Europe, Northern America, Southern America, and various Asian regions---and continent. We compute the ratio by dividing the fraction of same-geography edges by the fraction expected under random pairing. A ratio of 1.0 indicates geographic neutrality; values above 1.0 indicate geographic concentration. We choose the homophily ratio over the Newman assortativity coefficient $r$ because it is directly interpretable: a ratio of 4.85 means nominators and nominees shared a country 4.85 times more often than expected by chance. For a given edge set (fixed $E$), the two measures are monotonically related, with $r = (O - E)/(1 - E)$ where $H = O/E$; the corresponding assortativity for nomination edges is $r = 0.41$, confirming strong geographic sorting under either measure. We assess significance with 1,000-fold permutation tests that shuffle target geographies across edges while holding source geographies fixed, preserving the marginal geographic distributions of each \cite{methods}. This tests whether observed same-geography rates exceed what those marginal distributions alone would produce. The test does not condition on other covariates such as discipline overlap or institutional ties. Full construction details, subregion definitions, and sensitivity checks appear in the supplement.

\subsection*{Homophily Concentrates at the Nomination Edge}

The governing and vetting bodies are nearly entirely European, at 97.6\% and 98.9\% respectively, while the nominator, nominee, and laureate layers are more diverse, with 65.7--71.3\% European representation. The effective number of subregions---a diversity measure where higher values indicate representation across more geographic regions---ranges from 5.80 to 6.30 for the nominator, nominee, and laureate layers, compared with 1.23--1.65 for institutional layers. Despite this compositional contrast, geographic homophily concentrates in a single edge type: the nominator $\to$ nominee relationship (Fig.~\ref{fig:edge_homophily}).

At the country level, the nominator $\to$ nominee homophily ratio is 4.85, with $p < 0.001$: nominators and their nominees share the same country nearly five times more often than expected. The 95\% null reference interval under random pairing is [0.96, 1.03]---narrow because of the large sample of 25,844 edges---so the observed ratio lies far outside the range expected under geographic independence. The observed same-country rate is 46.8\%, versus 9.6\% expected. For context, geographic homophily in social networks is pervasive but typically moderate \cite{McPSmoCoo2001}, and recent work on journal peer review finds that same-country reviewers recommend acceptance at moderately elevated rates \cite{DumTep2025}; a ratio of 4.85 indicates exceptionally strong geographic clustering by comparison. A robustness check using birth country from Wikidata, available for 27.3\% of individuals, yields a ratio of 3.82 with $p < 0.001$. This is lower, as expected when substituting birth country for professional affiliation, but still far above neutral. The primary finding survives Bonferroni correction across our approximately 46 independent tests, giving $\alpha_{\mathrm{adjusted}} \approx 0.001$. Secondary findings at the discipline and decade level should be interpreted with appropriate caution.

Institutional edge types show ratios near 1.0 at the continent level: governing body $\to$ laureate at 1.01, vetting body $\to$ nominator at 1.00 with $p = 1.000$, and vetting body $\to$ laureate at 1.00 with $p = 1.000$. Because we model institutional edges as connecting all active members to all recipients in a given year, these near-1.0 ratios are expected by construction: within each prize-year bipartite block, every source connects to every target, so $O$ and $E$ converge and $H$ is forced toward 1.0 regardless of actual selection behavior. These ratios therefore serve as a compositional sanity check rather than a direct test of institutional selection neutrality. The one exception is the governing body $\to$ vetting body edge, which has a country-level ratio of 1.94. This arises because edges are pooled across multiple prize-awarding institutions (RSAS, Karolinska, Swedish Academy, Storting), each of which appoints its own national committee members---Sweden's RSAS members appoint Swedish committee members, for example---so same-country rates in the pooled edge set exceed what the combined marginal distributions alone would predict.

Stronger evidence about institutional behavior comes from the \emph{composition} of the nominator pool itself. Despite the vetting body being almost entirely Scandinavian, the nominator pool is geographically diverse, with an effective number of subregions of 6.30 compared to 1.23 for the vetting body. Institutional solicitation did not bottleneck geographic diversity. Geographic sorting instead appears one step later, when those diverse nominators choose whom to nominate.

\subsection*{Homophily Varies Across Disciplines}

Geographic homophily varies across prize categories in a pattern consistent with each discipline's international integration. At the country level, Literature exhibits the strongest homophily at 8.58, all $p < 0.001$, followed by Peace at 5.26, Physiology/Medicine at 5.16, Chemistry at 3.55, and Physics at 3.01. Because Physiology/Medicine data end in 1953 while other prizes extend to 1974--1975, and because homophily changes over time, we verified this ranking on a harmonized 1901--1953 window. The broad ordering is preserved---Literature highest (9.81), the natural sciences lowest (Chemistry 3.48, Physics 3.37)---with Physiology/Medicine and Peace essentially tied in the middle (5.16 and 5.15). The ordering follows a clear logic: literature depends on language, which correlates with geography, while physics and chemistry employ mathematical formalisms that cross borders. Within-subregion nomination rates reveal geographic heterogeneity: in Literature, Southern European nominators direct 92.7\% of their nominations within Southern Europe, while in Physics the figure drops to 24.9\%. Northern American nominators show intermediate behavior, directing 81.4\% of their Physiology/Medicine nominations within Northern America but only 44.4\% in Literature. Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap} displays these rates by prize and subregion.

\subsection*{Temporal Dynamics: Homophily Rises and Persists Despite Diversification}

The nominee pool diversified substantially: the European share fell from 94.2\% in the 1900s to 55.7\% in the 1970s, and the effective number of continents rose from 1.28 to 2.52. Fig.~\ref{fig:temporal}, panel~A, and Table~S5 display these trends. Because Physiology/Medicine nomination data end in 1953, the set of prizes contributing to the aggregate time series changes after that point; analyses of individual prize categories in the supplement confirm that each shows rising continent-level homophily. Despite this diversification, the continent-level homophily ratio \emph{increased}, from 1.07 in the 1900s to 1.54 in the 1940s, stabilizing at 1.43--1.45 through the 1970s, all with $p < 0.001$. Fig.~\ref{fig:temporal}, panel~B, shows this trend.

This pattern arises partly from the mechanics of the ratio: the absolute same-continent nomination rate decreased from 95.6\% to 65.6\%, but the expected rate under random pairing decreased faster, from 89.0\% to 45.7\%. As a result, $H = O/E$ rises even if nominators' underlying geographic preferences remain constant. In the early decades, the nominee pool was so overwhelmingly European that nearly any nomination would pair same-continent individuals by chance, leaving little room for large homophily ratios. A margin-robust measure, $O - E$ (the absolute excess same-continent rate), provides complementary evidence: $O - E$ rose from 6.6 percentage points in the 1900s to 27.0 in the 1940s before declining to 19.9 by the 1970s (Table~S5). The rise through the 1940s indicates that the increasing $H$ during that period reflects a genuine widening of the gap between observed and expected rates, not merely a shrinking denominator. The post-1940s decline in $O - E$, combined with a stable $H$, confirms that the later plateau in $H$ is partly a ratio artifact: observed and expected rates fell in approximate proportion, keeping $H$ roughly constant even as the absolute excess narrowed. The 1940s peak coincides with World War II disruptions to international scientific communication, which may have amplified geographic clustering in available information about candidates. This elevated ratio persisted from the 1950s onward even as international science recovered, suggesting that wartime constraints may have had lasting effects on nomination networks. Each prize category individually shows rising continent-level homophily, with the sharpest increases in the natural sciences during the 1930s--1940s.

\subsection*{Discussion}

The multilayer structure of this analysis is essential to its central conclusion. Prior single-layer studies could detect nomination homophily \cite{GalDom2019} but could not determine whether the Scandinavian-led institutions were the source: perhaps institutions preferentially invited European nominators, who then appeared to show homophily simply because the pool was geographically skewed. Our multilayer decomposition resolves this ambiguity through two independent lines of evidence. First, the nominator pool is geographically diverse despite being assembled by nearly all-Scandinavian institutions---effective number of subregions 6.30, versus 1.23 for the vetting body---establishing that institutional solicitation does not bottleneck diversity. Second, within that diverse pool, nominators show strong same-country preference: 4.85 at the country level, 1.43--1.54 at the continent level. Because institutional edges are modeled as complete bipartite connections, their near-1.0 ratios confirm compositional expectations but cannot rule out subtler institutional gatekeeping. The stronger claim is compositional: the institutions assembled a diverse nominator pool, and it is within that pool that geographic concentration appears.

What drives nominators to favor geographically proximate nominees? Three mechanisms are plausible. First, \emph{visibility}: scientists are more familiar with work produced in their own country, read by their own colleagues, and presented at their own conferences. Second, \emph{epistemic affinity}: nominators may regard work in their own tradition as higher quality, not from conscious partiality but because their evaluative frameworks were shaped by the same intellectual context. Third, \emph{strategic nationalism}: nominators may advocate for compatriots to enhance national scientific prestige. Our data cannot distinguish among these mechanisms, but the discipline gradient provides suggestive evidence. Literature, bound to language and national traditions, shows the strongest homophily at 8.58$\times$, while physics and chemistry, whose formalisms are internationally legible, show the weakest at 3.01$\times$ and 3.55$\times$. This gradient is more consistent with visibility and epistemic affinity than with strategic nationalism, which would not obviously vary across disciplines. It is also consistent with the geography of knowledge production: the spatial organization of intellectual activity shapes what scientists encounter \cite{Livingstone2003}, and stratification in science operates through cumulative small selection decisions \cite{ColCol1973, Mer1968}.

One partial corrective operates at the aggregate level: the median laureate received nominations from four distinct countries, with a range of 1 to 20, so the system's reliance on many nominators dilutes any single geographic preference. Nevertheless, geographic homophily generates systematic imbalances in the flow of recognition, as shown in Fig.~S1. Northern America is the largest net importer of nominations by volume, with a return ratio (nominations received / nominations sent) of 1.44, while Northern Europe---the administering region---is the largest net exporter by volume, with a return ratio of 0.73.

Several limitations warrant emphasis. The 50-year secrecy rule restricts our data to 1901--1975; we cannot determine whether patterns have persisted, though the temporal trend shows no sign of diminishing by the end of the observation window. The underlying mechanisms---information asymmetry, professional networks, cumulative advantage---have not disappeared from science, and recent evidence of geographic sorting in peer review \cite{DumTep2025} suggests these patterns extend beyond the Nobel system and beyond the mid-twentieth century. Worst-case sensitivity bounds remain far above the permutation null: assuming all 2,114 records with missing geography are cross-country yields $H = 4.48$, a 7.6\% reduction; treating all 432 historically recoded same-country matches as spurious yields $H = 4.68$, a 3.6\% reduction. The homophily ratio $H = O/E$ is mechanically sensitive to the magnitude of $E$: as the pool diversifies and $E$ shrinks, $H$ can rise even without changes in underlying preference. The margin-robust measure $O - E$ (Table~S5) shows that the absolute excess same-continent rate rose through the 1940s and then declined, indicating that the post-1940s plateau in $H$ partly reflects ratio mechanics rather than sustained behavioral intensification. We note that our analysis measures geographic sorting---not bias per se. Homophily patterns could partly reflect geographic differences in research visibility, information access, or institutional networks rather than preference bias; separating these mechanisms would require external data on research output by geography, beyond the scope of this study. Finally, we focus on geography to the exclusion of race, gender, and ethnicity; although we report gender composition by layer in Table~S6, female representation is so low, ranging from 2.3\% to 11.1\%, that gender-based analyses are uninformative, and race and ethnicity are not systematically recorded in historical sources.

These findings carry broader implications. The Nobel Prize shapes careers, funding, and public perceptions of scientific excellence \cite{CasFan2013, But2016}, so understanding where geographic sorting enters the selection process has consequences beyond the prize itself. Expanding the geographic diversity of the nominee pool alone is insufficient if nomination behavior does not change proportionally \cite{Nature2022}. Any evaluation system in which individual experts exercise judgment---science prizes, funding panels, editorial boards---appears susceptible to geographic concentration, regardless of whether the evaluators themselves are geographically diverse. By decomposing the Nobel selection process into a multilayer network, we show that geographic sorting is concentrated at a single point---the discretionary nomination decision---rather than in formal institutional design. This suggests that reducing geographic concentration requires interventions targeting nominator behavior---structured nomination procedures, explicit instructions to consider international candidates, cognitive debiasing---alongside continued efforts to diversify who is invited to nominate. More broadly, our multilayer decomposition offers a transferable methodology: by representing each institutional stage as a separate edge type, researchers can localize geographic sorting in other recognition systems---major science prizes, national academies, funding panels, editorial boards---and determine whether it arises from institutional policy or individual evaluator behavior.

\paragraph*{Ethics and positionality:} This study analyzes publicly archived Nobel Prize nomination records and published biographical data; no human subjects research was conducted. We acknowledge that the authors represent institutions and nations well-represented in the Nobel system (US and Sweden), which may shape our interpretation and introduce blind spots regarding underrepresented perspectives. We explicitly caution against interpreting findings of geographic homophily as evidence that geographic outsiders are inherently less deserving of recognition. Geographic clustering in nominations reflects the cumulative effects of past recognition patterns, which institutions can actively counteract through diversified nominator recruitment and structured nomination procedures.
