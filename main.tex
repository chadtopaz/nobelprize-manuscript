\begin{abstract}
Five countries account for over 80\% of Nobel Prize--winning discoveries, but whether this concentration reflects institutional design or individual evaluator behavior is unknown. We decompose Nobel selection into a multilayer network---governing bodies, vetting bodies, nominators, nominees, and laureates---spanning 8,134 individuals, 514,111 edges, and five prize categories (1901--1975). The predominantly Scandinavian institutions assembled a geographically diverse nominator pool, yet nominators select same-country candidates 4.85 times as often as expected ($p < 0.001$), ranging from 8.58$\times$ in Literature to 3.01$\times$ in Physics. This concentration persisted even as the nominee pool diversified. Geographic sorting thus concentrates at the discretionary nomination decision, not institutional design, identifying a specific intervention point for broadening scientific recognition.
\end{abstract}

\noindent The Nobel Prize is the most visible recognition system in science \cite{CasFan2013}, yet men from Europe or North America have received the vast majority of prizes \cite{LunJenJau2019, ChaEllRah2011, Cra2001}, and just five countries account for over 80\% of prize-winning discoveries \cite{vonZed2025}. Geographic concentration reinforces cumulative advantage \cite{Mer1968}: dominant regions accumulate resources, talent, and further recognition \cite{Zuc1977, ColCol1973, Gar1972}, while racial and national disparities compound the pattern \cite{NeiVueVue2024}. Whether these disparities arise from how candidates are selected or from where scientific output is produced remains unclear, yet this distinction determines whether reform should target evaluator behavior or the upstream production of potential candidates for selection. Homophily, the tendency for similar individuals to form ties \cite{McPSmoCoo2001}, offers a way to identify where geographic sorting enters the selection process: comparing observed same-geography rates to expected rates at each stage of a selection chain reveals where geographic sorting enters.

The architecture of the Nobel system offers an opportunity to localize this sorting. The process comprises multiple layers: governing bodies that set policy, vetting bodies that solicit nominators, nominators who submit candidates, and deliberating committees that select laureates \cite{methods}. Each layer could introduce or amplify concentration. Prior work shows that nominations cluster by country \cite{GalDom2019}, that same-country peer reviewers recommend acceptance more often \cite{ZumTep2025}, and that laureates occupy central positions in collaborative networks \cite{WagHorWhe2015}. Gendered barriers further constrain who enters the nomination pipeline \cite{ModGilSha2018, LunJenJau2019}. But single-layer analyses of the nominator--nominee relationship \cite{GalDom2019} cannot determine whether observed clustering reflects nominator preferences or the committees' selection of nominators---if the all-Scandinavian committees disproportionately invited nominators from a narrow set of countries, the resulting nominations would cluster by country even without nominators' individual geographic preference. Regression-based approaches \cite{KoJanYan2024} face a similar identification problem.

We address this gap by decomposing the selection process into a multilayer network \cite{Kivela2014} with separate edge sets for each decision point. If the all-Scandinavian committees were the primary source of geographic sorting, we would expect a geographically skewed nominator pool---committees disproportionately inviting Scandinavian nominators. If nominators were the primary source, we would expect a diverse nominator pool, but a concentration in the nominations those individuals make. We assess which pattern the data support using two complementary approaches: comparing the geographic composition of each layer and computing homophily ratios for each edge type. Although interdependencies exist between layers---committee composition shapes the available nominator pool, for instance---our focus on direct edge homophily provides a clear decomposition of the selection chain.

\subsection*{Network Construction and Homophily Measurement}

We construct a directed multilayer network spanning all five original Nobel Prize categories---Chemistry, Physics, Physiology/Medicine, Literature, and Peace---from 1901 to 1974--1975, the most recent publicly available years under Sweden's 50-year secrecy rule (Fig.~\ref{fig:schematic}). The network has five layers: governing bodies, vetting bodies, nominators, nominees, and laureates. We refer to governing bodies and vetting bodies as institutional layers. Nomination edges connect nominator--nominee pairs where a nomination occurred. 
Temporal coverage varies by prize: Physiology/Medicine nomination records extend only through 1953 because the Karolinska Institutet has not released later records; institutional-layer data extend further. We exclude the Economics Prize, established in 1969, because of its different institutional structure and limited archival overlap. 
We georeferenced individuals from two sources: the nomination layer uses the country of professional activity from the archival record, while institutional layers use the birth country from Wikidata.

We assembled the network from archival records, biographical databases, and scholarly literature spanning 81 countries; it comprises 8,134 individuals and 514,111 directed edges: 26,654 nominations and 487,457 institutional.
We model relationships among the five layers as complete bipartite connections (every member of one body linked to every member of the next) within each year and prize category, reflecting their collective nature. This construction means that network-level homophily ratios primarily reflect the composition of the network rather than preferences for same-group connections, a point we return to below. 

To measure geographic sorting, we compute a homophily ratio $H = O/E$ for each edge type at three scales: country, subregion---geographic clusters such as Northern Europe, Southern Europe, and Northern America---and continent. The ratio divides the observed fraction of same-geography edges ($O$) by the fraction expected under random pairing ($E$). A ratio of 1.0 indicates geographic neutrality; values above 1.0 indicate concentration. This null model is a statistical benchmark for detecting excess same-geography pairing given the observed marginal distributions, not a claim that scientific capacity is geographically uniform; a ratio exceeding 1.0 could reflect geographic differences in research visibility, evaluator preference, or both. To preview the headline result: nominators and nominees share a country 4.85 times more often than expected by chance, with a corresponding Newman assortativity of $r = 0.41$ \cite{New2003}, comparable to racial assortativity in social networks \cite{McPSmoCoo2001}. We assess significance with 10,000-fold permutation tests that shuffle target geographies while holding source geographies fixed, preserving the marginal distributions \cite{methods}. Stratified analyses by discipline and decade appear in the supplement, along with full construction details, subregion definitions, and sensitivity checks.

\subsection*{Homophily Concentrates at the Nomination Edge}

The governing and vetting bodies are nearly entirely European (97.6\% and 98.9\%), with 84.7\% and 96.3\% born in Sweden, Norway, or Denmark, while the nominator, nominee, and laureate layers are more diverse, at 65.7--71.3\% European. The effective number of subregions, a diversity measure where higher values indicate broader representation, ranges from 5.80 to 6.30 for the nominator (6.30), nominee (5.80), and laureate layers (5.97), versus 1.23--1.65 for institutional layers. Despite this compositional contrast, geographic homophily concentrates in a single edge type: the nominator $\to$ nominee relationship (Fig.~\ref{fig:edge_homophily}).

At the country level, the nominator $\to$ nominee homophily ratio is 4.85 ($p < 0.001$): nominators and their nominees share a country nearly five times more often than expected. The 95\% null reference interval under random pairing is [0.97, 1.03], narrow because of the large sample of 25,844 edges, so the observed ratio lies far outside the range expected under geographic independence. The observed same-country rate is 46.8\%, versus 9.6\% expected. For context, geographic homophily in social networks is pervasive, but typically moderate \cite{McPSmoCoo2001}, and same-country peer reviewers recommend acceptance at only moderately elevated rates \cite{ZumTep2025}; a ratio of 4.85 indicates exceptionally strong clustering by comparison. A robustness check using birth country from Wikidata, available for 27.3\% of individuals, yields a ratio of 3.82 ($p < 0.001$)---lower, as expected, but far above neutral. This matched subset is not a random sample (individuals with Wikidata pages are disproportionately prominent), so its homophily characteristics may differ from the full population. The main country-level finding survives Bonferroni correction across 49 independent tests, with $\alpha_{\mathrm{adjusted}} = 0.05/49 \approx 0.001$. Blockwise permutations that shuffle within prize-year blocks, accounting for the stratified structure of the network, yield identical ratios and $p < 0.001$ at all geographic scales. Secondary findings at the discipline and decade level should be interpreted with appropriate caution.

Institutional edge types show ratios near 1.0 at the continent level: governing body $\to$ laureate at 1.01, vetting body $\to$ nominator at 1.00 ($p = 1.000$), and vetting body $\to$ laureate at 1.00 ($p = 1.000$). As expected from the complete bipartite construction, these near-1.0 ratios serve as a compositional sanity check rather than a direct test of institutional selection neutrality. At the country level, most institutional edges similarly cluster near 1.0. The exception is the governing body $\to$ vetting body edge, with a ratio of 1.65, which arises because we pool edges across multiple prize-awarding institutions (RSAS, Karolinska, Swedish Academy, Storting), each of which appoints its own national committee members, so same-country rates in the pooled set exceed what the combined marginal distributions alone would predict. Because institutional-laureate edges extend through 2025 while nomination data end at 1974--1975, these comparisons span different temporal windows; restricting institutional-laureate edges to the nomination window yields ratios of 1.10 (country) and 1.01 (continent), leaving all findings unchanged.

Stronger evidence comes from the \emph{composition} of the nominator pool. Despite the nearly all-Scandinavian vetting body, the nominator pool is geographically diverse, with an effective number of subregions of 6.30 versus 1.23 for the vetting body. Institutional solicitation did not bottleneck geographic diversity; sorting occurs one step later, when those diverse nominators choose whom to nominate. Homophily in the nominator layer structures nomination flows and limits increases in nominee diversity. Along with asymmetric regional flows, this produces a small decline in diversity in the nominee layer.  

\subsection*{Homophily Varies Across Disciplines}

Geographic homophily varies across prize categories. At the country level, Literature exhibits the strongest homophily at 8.58, all $p < 0.001$, followed by Peace at 5.26, Physiology/Medicine at 5.16, Chemistry at 3.55, and Physics at 3.01. Because Physiology/Medicine data end in 1953 while other prizes extend to 1974--1975, and because homophily changes over time, we verified this ranking on a harmonized 1901--1953 window. The broad ordering is preserved---Literature highest at 9.81, the physical sciences lowest at 3.48 and 3.37---with Physiology/Medicine and Peace essentially tied in the middle at 5.16 and 5.15. This ordering tracks how much evaluation in each field depends on shared language and cultural context: literary merit is inseparable from the language in which a work is written, while assessment in physics and chemistry relies more on standardized technical criteria---though even these fields retain geographic structure in training, collaboration, and professional networks. Within-subregion nomination rates reveal further heterogeneity: in Literature, Southern European nominators direct 92.7\% of their nominations within Southern Europe, versus 24.9\% in Physics. Northern American nominators show intermediate behavior, directing 81.4\% of their Physiology/Medicine nominations within Northern America but only 44.4\% in Literature. Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap} displays these rates by prize and subregion.

\subsection*{Temporal Dynamics: Homophily Rises and Persists Despite Diversification}

The nominee pool diversified substantially: the European share fell from 94.2\% in the 1900s to 55.7\% in the 1970s, and the effective number of continents rose from 1.28 to 2.52 (Fig.~\ref{fig:temporal}A,B; Table~S5). Because Physiology/Medicine nomination data end in 1953, the set of prizes contributing to the aggregate time series changes after that point; analyses of individual prize categories in the supplement confirm that each shows rising continent-level homophily. Despite this diversification, the continent-level homophily ratio \emph{increased}, from 1.07 in the 1900s to 1.54 in the 1940s, then stabilized at 1.43--1.45 through the 1970s (all $p < 0.001$; Fig.~\ref{fig:temporal}C).

This pattern arises partly from ratio mechanics: the absolute same-continent nomination rate decreased from 95.6\% to 65.6\%, but the expected rate under random pairing decreased faster, from 89.0\% to 45.7\%. As a result, $H = O/E$ rises even if underlying geographic preferences remain constant. In the early decades, the nominee pool was so overwhelmingly European that nearly any nomination would pair individuals from the same continent by chance, leaving little room for large homophily ratios.

An absolute measure tells a clearer story. The excess same-continent rate, $O - E$, rose from 6.6 percentage points in the 1900s to 27.0 in the 1940s before declining to 19.9 by the 1970s (Table~S5). The rise through the 1940s shows that increasing $H$ during that period reflects a genuine widening of the gap between observed and expected rates, not merely a shrinking denominator. The post-1940s decline in $O - E$, combined with stable $H$, confirms that the later plateau is partly a ratio artifact: observed and expected rates fell in approximate proportion. The 1940s peak coincides with World War II disruptions to international scientific communication, which may have amplified geographic clustering. This elevated ratio persisted from the 1950s onward, even as international science recovered, though we cannot determine whether wartime disruptions specifically caused the shift or merely coincided with it. Each prize category individually shows rising continent-level homophily, with the sharpest increases in the natural sciences during the 1930s--1940s.

\subsection*{Discussion}

Without observing each layer separately, it is impossible to distinguish nominator preference from institutional gatekeeping: if institutions preferentially invited nominators from a narrow set of countries, nominations would cluster by country even without individual geographic preference. Our multilayer decomposition resolves this ambiguity through two independent lines of evidence. First, the nominator pool is geographically diverse despite being assembled by predominantly Scandinavian institutions---effective number of subregions 6.30, versus 1.23 for the vetting body---establishing that institutional solicitation does not bottleneck diversity. Second, within that diverse pool, nominators show strong same-country preference: 4.85 at the country level, 1.43--1.54 at the continent level. Because we model institutional edges as complete bipartite connections, their near-1.0 ratios confirm compositional expectations but cannot rule out subtler institutional gatekeeping; a more targeted test would require data on individual committee members' votes or influence. The stronger claim is compositional: the institutions assembled a diverse nominator pool, and it is within that pool that geographic concentration appears. Newman assortativity on the full supra-adjacency matrix confirms this asymmetry: at the continent level, nomination edges yield $r = 0.30$ while institutional edges yield $r = 0.02$ \cite{methods}. A formal layer-contrast permutation test, which randomly reassigns edge-type labels while preserving the number of edges in each class, confirms that this difference is significant ($\Delta H = 2.37$, $p < 0.001$; using birth country throughout for a consistent geographic definition) \cite{methods}. A high level of homophily at this stage limits improvements in the diversity of the nominee pool. Combined with asymmetric nomination flows---where some regions nominate mostly internally while others direct nominations toward scientifically dominant regions---this results in a nominee pool that is slightly less diverse than the nominator pool. 
Cross-layer comparisons should be interpreted with care because the nomination layer uses the country of professional activity from archival records while institutional layers use birth country from Wikidata. A robustness check using birth country for all layers yields a nomination homophily ratio of 3.82 ($p < 0.001$), confirming the core finding under a consistent geographic definition. A still stronger test would require the invitation lists themselves---who was asked to nominate, not just who submitted---but these records are not publicly available.

What drives nominators to favor geographically proximate nominees? Academic evaluators rely on disciplinary norms, personal networks, and tacit knowledge of what counts as excellence \cite{Lam2009}, and all three channels are geographically structured. Three mechanisms are plausible: \emph{visibility}, since knowledge flows are geographically localized even after controlling for the spatial distribution of activity \cite{JafTraHen1993}; \emph{epistemic affinity}, where evaluative frameworks shaped by the same intellectual context lead nominators to regard familiar work as higher quality; and \emph{strategic nationalism}, where nominators advocate for compatriots to enhance national prestige. The discipline gradient provides suggestive evidence: Literature, where evaluation depends on shared language and cultural context, shows the strongest homophily (8.58$\times$), while physics and chemistry show the weakest (3.01$\times$ and 3.55$\times$). This gradient is more consistent with visibility and epistemic affinity than with strategic nationalism, which would not obviously vary across disciplines---though confounds including differences in nomination rules and field size could produce similar patterns. The spatial organization of intellectual activity shapes what scientists encounter \cite{Livingstone2003, Sha1994}, and stratification in science operates through cumulative small selection decisions \cite{ColCol1973, Mer1968, deSollaPrice1963}.

The system's reliance on many nominators provides a partial corrective---the median laureate received nominations from four distinct countries \cite{nobelgithub}---but geographic homophily still generates systematic imbalances: Northern America is the largest net importer of nominations (return ratio 1.44), while Northern Europe is the largest net exporter (0.73; Fig.~S1).

This study has important limitations. The 50-year secrecy rule restricts our data to 1901--1975; we cannot determine whether patterns have persisted, though the temporal trend shows no sign of diminishing by the end of the observation window, and recent evidence of geographic sorting in peer review \cite{ZumTep2025} suggests these patterns extend beyond the Nobel system. A clustered permutation test that shuffles geography labels at the nominator level, preserving within-nominator correlation, confirms that edge dependence does not affect inference ($p < 0.001$) \cite{methods}. Worst-case sensitivity bounds remain far above the permutation null ($H \geq 4.48$; see supplement). Our vetting body $\to$ nominator analysis assumes geographic non-response is not systematic; differential non-response could bias the observed diversity of the nominator pool in either direction.

Beyond these statistical considerations, our analysis measures geographic sorting---not bias per se. What counts as excellent work is shaped by local intellectual traditions, not only by who evaluates it, and homophily patterns could partly reflect geographic differences in research visibility or institutional networks rather than preference bias; separating these mechanisms would require external data on research output by geography, beyond the scope of this study. We focus on geography because it is the one identity dimension for which we have near-complete data across all layers, but geographic disparities may partly proxy for racial, linguistic, or institutional hierarchies we cannot disentangle with these data. Gender and racial disparities in the Nobel system are well documented \cite{LunJenJau2019, NeiVueVue2024} and warrant separate investigation with appropriate data.

The Nobel Prize shapes careers, funding, and public perceptions of scientific excellence \cite{CasFan2013, But2016}, so identifying where geographic sorting enters the selection process has consequences beyond the prize itself. Expanding the geographic diversity of the nominee pool alone is insufficient if nomination behavior does not change proportionally \cite{Nature2022}. The clustering we observe suggests that evaluation systems in which individual experts exercise judgment---science prizes, funding panels, editorial boards---may be susceptible to geographic concentration even when the evaluators themselves are geographically diverse. Because sorting concentrates at the discretionary nomination decision rather than in formal institutional design, reducing geographic concentration requires interventions targeting nominator behavior---structured nomination procedures, explicit instructions to consider international candidates, cognitive debiasing---alongside continued efforts to diversify who is invited to nominate. The multilayer decomposition we introduce is transferable to other recognition systems wherever the question is whether geographic sorting arises from institutional policy or individual evaluator behavior.
