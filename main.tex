\begin{abstract}
\bfseries \boldmath
The Nobel Prize exhibits stark geographic concentration---five countries produced over 80\% of laureates---but whether this reflects institutional design or individual evaluator behavior has been unknown. By integrating archival nomination records with institutional composition data, we construct the first multilayer network of the Nobel selection process (8,134 individuals, 303,629 edges, five categories, 1901--1975), decomposing geographic homophily across five institutional layers. Institutional gatekeepers show negligible geographic bias (homophily ratios near 1.0), while nominators favor same-country candidates at 4.85 times the expected rate ($p < 0.001$). This concentration is strongest in Literature (8.58$\times$), weakest in Physics (3.01$\times$), and has intensified over time despite diversification of the nominee pool. Expanding candidate diversity alone has not been accompanied by proportional changes in nomination behavior.
\end{abstract}

\noindent The Nobel Prize is the most visible recognition system in science, yet the vast majority of laureates have been men from Europe or North America \cite{LunJenJau2019, Fis2013, Cra2001}, and just five countries produced over 80\% of prize-winning discoveries \cite{vonZed2025}. Geographic concentration reinforces cumulative advantage \cite{Mer1968}: prestige attracts further prestige, and dominant regions accumulate resources and talent \cite{Zuc1977}. However, it remains unknown whether these patterns reflect bias in the selection process or simply the geographic distribution of scientific output.

Homophily---the tendency for similar individuals to form ties---is a fundamental principle in social networks \cite{McPSmoCoo2001}. We measure observed geographic clustering against the expectation that scientific quality is distributed independently of geography. Higher ratios could reflect geographic differences in research capacity, visibility, or infrastructure---real phenomena worthy of study. Alternatively, they could reflect evaluator bias: preferential nomination of geographically proximate scholars. Even if geographic differences in output are real, preferential nomination compounds existing inequalities. Regardless of mechanism, the result is the same: the Nobel Prize selection process amplifies existing geographic disparities in scientific visibility and resources. Disentangling the source matters for two reasons. First, \emph{fairness}: scientists from underrepresented regions deserve equitable access to nomination and recognition. Second, \emph{epistemic integrity}: evaluation systems that preferentially amplify geographically proximate work miss contributions from elsewhere and produce distorted records of scientific progress.

The architecture of the Nobel system offers a unique opportunity to localize the source of geographic sorting. The process comprises multiple layers: governing bodies that set policy, vetting committees that solicit nominators, nominators who submit candidates, and deliberating committees that select laureates \cite{methods}. Each layer could introduce or amplify concentration. Prior work shows that nominators favor individuals from similar national backgrounds \cite{GalDom2019} and that same-country peer reviewers recommend acceptance more often \cite{DumTep2025}. But existing analyses---single-layer \cite{GalDom2019} or regression-based \cite{KoJanYan2024}---could not distinguish institutional sources from individual ones or isolate each layer's independent contribution.

We address this gap by decomposing the selection process into a multilayer network representation with separate edge sets for each decision point. This decomposition allows us to localize where geographic concentration appears in the system. If the all-Scandinavian committees were the primary source of bias, we would expect geographic concentration in institutional edges (e.g., committees disproportionately selecting Scandinavian nominators). If nominators were the primary source, we would expect concentration only in nomination edges. By analyzing each edge type independently and comparing homophily ratios across types, we assess which pattern the data support. Although interdependencies exist between layers (e.g., committee composition shapes the available nominator pool), our focus on direct edge homophily provides a clear, interpretable decomposition of the selection chain.

\subsection*{Network Construction and Homophily Measurement}

We construct a directed multilayer network spanning all five prize categories from 1901 to 1975, the most recent publicly available year due to the Swedish legal 50-year secrecy rule for official nomination archives (Fig.~\ref{fig:schematic}). The network has five layers: governing bodies, vetting bodies, nominators, nominees, and laureates. We model institutional relationships as complete bipartite connections within each year and prize category, reflecting their collective nature. Nomination edges connect nominator--nominee pairs where a nomination occurred. We assembled the network from archival records, biographical databases, and scholarly literature spanning 81 countries; it comprises 8,134 individuals and 303,629 directed edges (61,384 nomination; 242,245 institutional). For each edge type, we compute the homophily ratio at three geographic scales: country, subregion (geographic clusters such as Northern Europe, Southern Europe, Northern America, Southern America, and various Asian regions), and continent. We compute the ratio by dividing the fraction of same-geography edges by the fraction expected under random pairing. A ratio of 1.0 indicates geographic neutrality; values above 1.0 indicate geographic concentration. We choose the homophily ratio over alternative measures (assortativity, entropy-based indices) because it is directly interpretable: a ratio of 4.85 means nominators and nominees shared a country 4.85 times more often than expected by chance, making the effect size immediately accessible. We assess significance with 1,000-fold permutation tests \cite{methods}. Full construction details, subregion definitions, and sensitivity checks appear in the supplement.

\subsection*{Homophily Concentrates at the Nomination Edge}

The governing and vetting bodies are nearly entirely European (97.6\% and 98.9\%), while the nominator, nominee, and laureate layers are more diverse (65.7--71.3\% European; effective subregion count 5.80--6.30 vs.\ 1.23--1.65 for institutional layers). Despite this compositional contrast, geographic homophily concentrates in a single edge type: the nominator $\to$ nominee relationship (Fig.~\ref{fig:edge_homophily}).

At the country level, the nominator $\to$ nominee homophily ratio is 4.85 ($p < 0.001$; 95\% CI: [4.71, 4.99]): nominators and their nominees share the same country nearly five times more often than expected. The observed same-country rate is 46.8\%, versus 9.6\% expected. For context, empirical homophily ratios in large social networks typically range from 1.1 to 2.0; a ratio of 4.85 indicates exceptionally strong geographic clustering. A robustness check using birth country (from Wikidata, available for 27.3\% of individuals) yields a ratio of 3.82 ($p < 0.001$; 95\% CI: [3.68, 3.96]), lower as expected when substituting birth country for professional affiliation, but still far above neutral. The primary finding survives Bonferroni correction across our approximately 46 independent tests ($\alpha_{\mathrm{adjusted}} \approx 0.001$); we should interpret secondary findings (discipline- and decade-specific) with appropriate caution.

Institutional edge types show ratios near 1.0 at the continent level: governing body $\to$ laureate (1.01), vetting body $\to$ nominator (1.00, $p = 1.000$), and vetting body $\to$ laureate (1.00, $p = 1.000$), all indicating negligible geographic concentration. The one exception, governing body $\to$ vetting body (country-level ratio 1.94), reflects a structural tautology: both bodies are almost entirely Scandinavian by design, so high same-country rates follow from institutional composition rather than discretionary preference.

The vetting body $\to$ nominator edge is particularly informative. Despite the vetting body being almost entirely Scandinavian, the nominator pool is geographically diverse (effective subregion count 6.30). The same-country ratio for this edge is 1.01 ($p = 0.070$, not significant), providing weak evidence that the vetting body does not introduce geographic concentration when selecting nominators. Geographic sorting instead appears one step later, when those diverse nominators choose whom to nominate.

\subsection*{Homophily Varies Across Disciplines}

Geographic homophily varies across prize categories in a pattern consistent with each discipline's international integration. At the country level, Literature exhibits the strongest homophily (8.58, $p < 0.001$), followed by Peace (5.26), Physiology/Medicine (5.16), Chemistry (3.55), and Physics (3.01). This ordering follows a clear logic: literature depends on language, which correlates with geography, while physics and chemistry employ mathematical formalisms that cross borders. Within-subregion nomination rates reveal geographic heterogeneity: in Literature, Southern European nominators direct 92.7\% of their nominations within Southern Europe, while in Physics the figure drops to 24.9\%. Northern American nominators show intermediate behavior, nominating 81.4\% of other Northern Americans in Physiology/Medicine but only 44.4\% in Literature. Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap} displays these rates by prize and subregion.

\subsection*{Temporal Dynamics: Homophily Intensifies Despite Diversification}

The nominee pool diversified substantially: the European share fell from 94.2\% in the 1900s to 55.7\% in the 1970s, and the effective number of continents rose from 1.28 to 2.52 (Fig.~\ref{fig:temporal}, panel~A; Table~S5). Because Physiology/Medicine nomination data end in 1953, the set of prizes contributing to the aggregate time series changes after that point; analyses of individual prize categories in the supplement confirm that each shows rising continent-level homophily. Despite this diversification, the continent-level homophily ratio \emph{increased}, from 1.07 in the 1900s to 1.54 in the 1940s, stabilizing at 1.43--1.45 through the 1970s (Fig.~\ref{fig:temporal}, panel~B; all $p < 0.001$).

This apparent paradox arises because the absolute same-continent nomination rate decreased (from 95.6\% to 65.6\%) but the expected rate under random pairing decreased faster (from 89.0\% to 45.7\%). In the early decades, the nominee pool was so overwhelmingly European that nearly any nomination would pair same-continent individuals by chance, leaving little room for detectable homophily. As the pool diversified, the expected rate fell, but nomination behavior did not diversify at an equivalent pace, so the ratio rose. The 1940s peak likely reflects World War II disruptions to international scientific communication, which would amplify geographic clustering in available information about candidates. This peak persisted from the 1950s onward even as international science recovered, suggesting that wartime constraints may have had lasting effects on nomination networks. Each prize category individually shows rising continent-level homophily, with the sharpest increases in the natural sciences during the 1930s--1940s.

\subsection*{Discussion}

The central finding is what we do \emph{not} find in institutional edges. The nearly all-Scandinavian institutions show negligible geographic preference in their direct relational patterns (ratios near 1.0). Instead, nominators show strong homophily (4.85 at country level, 1.43--1.54 at continent level), suggesting that geographic concentration is associated with evaluator selections rather than institutional gatekeeping. This decomposition reveals that geographic concentration in this recognition system is localized not in formal institutional rules, but in the aggregated nomination decisions of individual experts. We emphasize important caveats: the geographic diversity of the nominator pool is itself shaped by institutional selection; homophily patterns could partly reflect geographic differences in research visibility rather than preference bias; and all analyses are constrained to the Euro-American dominance of the Nobel system (65.7--71.3\% European). Separating visibility constraints from preference biases would require external data on research output by geography, beyond the scope of this study. Because the nomination archive is subject to a 50-year secrecy rule, our data extend only through the mid-1970s, and we cannot determine whether these patterns have persisted or diminished.

The discipline gradient---Literature (8.58$\times$) versus Physics (3.01$\times$)---is consistent with information constraints: fields whose communicative medium is language-bound show stronger geographic sorting than those employing internationally legible formalisms. This aligns with foundational work in the sociology of science showing that stratification operates through the cumulative effects of small selection decisions \cite{ColCol1973}. However, we cannot rule out in-group preference or real geographic differences in research capacity as contributing factors.

One partial corrective operates at the aggregate level: the median laureate received nominations from four distinct countries (range 1--20), so the system's reliance on many nominators dilutes any single geographic preference. Nevertheless, systematic imbalances persist: Northern America is the largest net importer of nominations (return ratio 1.44), while Northern Europe---the administering region---is the largest net exporter (0.73; Fig.~S1).

\subsection*{Limitations}

Several limitations constrain our analysis. First, the 50-year secrecy rule restricts our data to 1901--1975; we cannot determine whether patterns have persisted or diminished. Second, our record-linkage process matched only 27.3\% of nomination archive individuals to Wikidata QIDs (86.5\% among those with birth years); the full nomination dataset provides per-nomination geographic fields at 94.7--97.7\% completeness. Third, the primary analysis uses country of professional activity; the robustness check uses birth country; neither captures migration trajectories or historical boundary changes. Fourth, our null model of random pairing does not assess whether institutional processes create geographic bias indirectly through nominator pool composition; we interpret the permutation test as measuring nominators' tendency to favor same-country colleagues given the pool they inhabit. Fifth, we conduct approximately 46 tests; although the primary finding survives Bonferroni correction, secondary results merit caution. Sixth, we focus on geography to the exclusion of race, gender, and ethnicity. Although we report gender composition by layer (Table~S6), female representation is so low (2.3--11.1\%) that gender homophily ratios are uninformative; race and ethnicity are not systematically recorded in historical sources and are socially constructed in ways that vary across cultures and periods. We cannot assess whether geographic homophily intersects with other forms of exclusion.

\subsection*{Conclusions}

The temporal finding carries a broader policy implication: expanding the geographic diversity of the nominee pool alone is insufficient if nomination behavior does not change proportionally. Any evaluation system in which individual experts exercise judgment---science prizes, funding panels, editorial boards---appears susceptible to geographic concentration, regardless of whether the evaluators themselves are geographically diverse. By decomposing the Nobel Prize selection process into a multilayer network, we show that geographic sorting is concentrated at a single point---the discretionary nomination decision---rather than in formal institutional design or policy. This finding suggests that reducing geographic bias requires two complementary interventions: (1) interventions targeting nominator behavior (structured nomination templates, blind review processes, cognitive debiasing), and (2) deliberate institutional efforts to diversify who becomes a nominator in the first place. The results demonstrate that the formal absence of geographic restrictions does not eliminate geographic bias; institutions must proactively reshape both nominator pools and nominator decision-making.
